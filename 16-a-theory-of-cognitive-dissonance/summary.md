# A Theory of Cognitive Dissonance (1957) — Festinger

In 1957, Leon Festinger proposed one of the most influential ideas in social psychology: when people simultaneously hold two contradictory beliefs, or when their behaviour clashes with their beliefs, they experience a deeply uncomfortable psychological tension he called cognitive dissonance. Festinger argued that this discomfort is not merely intellectual — it functions more like hunger or thirst, a motivational state that people are driven to reduce. The theory mattered because it overturned the commonsense assumption that attitudes guide behaviour. Festinger showed the arrow often points the other way: behaviour reshapes attitudes.

The most famous test of the theory was the "boring task" experiment. Participants performed a tedious peg-turning task for an hour, then were paid either $1 or $20 to tell the next participant that the task was enjoyable. Afterward, those paid only $1 rated the task as genuinely more interesting than those paid $20. The logic was counterintuitive but precise: participants who received $20 had sufficient external justification for lying, so they felt no tension. Those paid just $1 could not justify the lie with money, so their minds resolved the dissonance by actually shifting their belief — deciding the task was not so bad after all. The brain chose to rewrite the attitude rather than sit with the contradiction.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, cognitive dissonance explains a wide range of post-purchase phenomena. Buyers who spend heavily on a product often become its loudest advocates — not despite the cost but because of it, since admitting the purchase was unwise would create painful dissonance. This is why luxury brands benefit from high prices, why subscription services offer commitment tiers, and why post-purchase reassurance messaging is so effective. Marketers who understand dissonance can design experiences that lock in commitment: once consumers act, their beliefs follow, making brand loyalty a self-reinforcing cycle rather than something that must be constantly earned.
