# Defaults and Donation Decisions (2004) — Johnson & Goldstein

Eric Johnson and Daniel Goldstein examined why organ donation consent rates varied so dramatically across European countries with similar cultures and values. Austria had a consent rate of 99.98% while neighbouring Germany sat at just 12%, and Sweden reached 85.9% while Denmark managed only 4.25%. The question was whether these differences reflected genuine differences in public attitudes toward organ donation, or whether something far simpler was at work. Their hypothesis was that the structure of the decision — specifically which option was the default — was doing almost all the heavy lifting.

The researchers used both a controlled online experiment and real-world cross-country comparisons. In the experiment, participants were randomly assigned to one of three conditions: opt-in (must actively check a box to become a donor), opt-out (must actively check a box to remove themselves as a donor), or mandated choice (must select one option with neither pre-selected). The results were dramatic. Opt-in produced roughly 42% agreement to donate, mandated choice produced about 79%, and opt-out produced approximately 82%. The cross-country data reinforced this pattern almost perfectly — nations with opt-out (presumed consent) policies clustered near 100% donation rates, while opt-in nations clustered between 4% and 28%. Johnson and Goldstein identified three mechanisms: effort (any action, however small, reduces participation), implied endorsement (people interpret the default as the recommended or normal option), and constructed preferences (when people have no strong prior opinion, the default effectively creates their preference for them).

For marketing and behavioural design, this study is foundational. It demonstrates that for any decision where consumers lack strong pre-existing preferences — which describes most low-engagement purchases, subscription choices, and privacy settings — the default option becomes the dominant determinant of behaviour. This is why pre-ticked subscription boxes increase sign-ups, why auto-enrolment transforms pension participation, and why app permission defaults shape data-sharing rates. The ethical dimension is equally important: because defaults function as invisible architecture, whoever sets the default holds enormous power over outcomes, making default design one of the most consequential and least scrutinised tools in consumer behaviour.
