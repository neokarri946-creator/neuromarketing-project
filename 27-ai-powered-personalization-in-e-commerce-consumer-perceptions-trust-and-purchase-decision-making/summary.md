# AI-Powered Personalization in E-Commerce: Consumer Perceptions, Trust, and Purchase Decision-Making (2025)

Online retailers increasingly use artificial intelligence to personalise what shoppers see — tailored product recommendations, customised homepage layouts, and individually targeted promotions. But does this personalisation actually make people buy more, and when does it backfire? This 2025 study investigated the critical relationship between AI-driven personalisation, consumer trust, and purchase decision-making, a question that matters because billions of pounds in e-commerce revenue now depend on recommendation algorithms whose psychological effects are poorly understood.

The research found that AI personalisation does increase purchase likelihood — but only when consumers trust the system delivering the recommendations. Trust acts as a gateway: when shoppers believe the AI understands their genuine preferences and is working in their interest, personalised suggestions feel helpful and relevant, boosting both engagement and conversion. However, the study identified a crucial threshold effect. When personalisation becomes too precise — referencing information consumers did not knowingly share, predicting desires they had not expressed, or appearing to "know too much" — it triggers what researchers call the "creepiness effect." This over-personalisation produces a sharp drop in trust that outweighs any benefit from recommendation accuracy. The relationship between personalisation intensity and purchase intention follows an inverted U-shape: too little personalisation feels generic and irrelevant, too much feels invasive and unsettling, and the optimum sits in a middle zone where recommendations feel insightful but not surveillance-like.

For e-commerce practitioners, the findings carry a direct warning: more data and more precise targeting do not automatically produce better outcomes. The optimal strategy involves personalising enough to demonstrate relevance while maintaining boundaries that preserve the consumer's sense of autonomy and privacy. Transparency about how recommendations are generated — explaining "we suggest this because you bought X" rather than silently inferring preferences — helps sustain trust. Brands that chase maximum personalisation without monitoring the creepiness threshold risk converting a powerful sales tool into a source of consumer anxiety and avoidance.
