# How Context Alters Value: The Brain's Valuation and Affective Regulation System (2017) — Schmidt et al.

Why does the same coffee feel like a bargain at a motorway service station but overpriced at a corner shop? Schmidt and colleagues investigated how the brain's valuation system recalculates the perceived worth of an item depending on what surrounds it. This question mattered because classical economic theory assumes people assign stable, context-independent values to goods — yet everyday experience and mounting behavioural evidence suggested that perceived value shifts dramatically with the comparison set. Understanding the neural mechanism behind this shift would reveal whether context merely influences reporting or genuinely rewires how the brain computes worth.

Using fMRI, the researchers presented participants with consumer products alongside varying sets of alternatives — sometimes surrounded by cheaper items, sometimes by more expensive ones. They found that activity in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC), the brain's core valuation hub, systematically shifted depending on context. The same product triggered stronger valuation signals when placed among inferior alternatives and weaker signals when placed among superior ones. Critically, regions involved in affective regulation — including the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex — modulated these context-driven shifts, suggesting the brain actively adjusts emotional responses to maintain coherent decision-making rather than passively reflecting external information.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, these findings explain why retailers carefully control what appears alongside a target product. Placing a mid-range television next to a premium model makes the mid-range option feel like a smart deal, not because its features changed but because the brain literally recalculates its value downward relative to the anchor. This is the neural basis of decoy pricing, tiered product lines, and strategic shelf placement. It also warns consumers that their sense of "good value" is not an objective assessment — it is a context-dependent computation that can be engineered by anyone who controls the comparison set.
