# Revolutionizing Consumer Insights: The Impact of fMRI in Neuromarketing Research (2024) — Alsharif et al.

By the early 2020s, functional magnetic resonance imaging had been used in marketing research for over two decades, yet no comprehensive review had systematically mapped how fMRI had actually advanced the field. Alsharif and colleagues filled this gap in 2024, asking a straightforward question: what has fMRI concretely contributed to our understanding of consumer decision-making, and what practical barriers still limit its use? This mattered because the field had grown rapidly but unevenly, with some areas producing robust findings while others remained speculative. A clear stocktake was overdue.

The review synthesised findings across multiple domains of consumer behaviour and identified the key brain regions consistently implicated in purchasing and preference decisions. The prefrontal cortex emerged as central to evaluating product value and making trade-offs between options. The ventral striatum, including the nucleus accumbens, consistently tracked reward anticipation and desire. The insula signalled aversion and the pain of paying. The amygdala processed emotional associations with brands and advertisements. Critically, the review showed that these regions do not work in isolation — consumer decisions emerge from dynamic networks of interacting areas. The authors also documented practical challenges: fMRI is expensive, requires participants to lie motionless in a scanner, has limited temporal resolution compared to EEG, and produces data that requires sophisticated statistical analysis vulnerable to false positives if not handled carefully.

For practitioners and students of consumer behaviour, this review serves as a map of the neural geography of consumer decisions. It demonstrates that fMRI has moved beyond novelty to produce replicable findings about how the brain processes price, brand, advertising, and product choice. However, it also makes clear that neuroimaging is a complement to traditional marketing research, not a replacement. The most productive approach combines neural data with behavioural experiments and survey methods, using each to compensate for the others' blind spots. The practical future of neuromarketing lies in this integration, not in brain scans alone.
