# Behavioral Study of Obedience (1963) — Milgram

In the aftermath of the Holocaust, the world struggled with a disturbing question: were the people who carried out atrocities fundamentally different from ordinary citizens, or could normal individuals be led to commit terrible acts under the right circumstances? Stanley Milgram designed his obedience experiments at Yale University to answer this question with empirical precision. The stakes were enormous — if ordinary people could be systematically directed to harm others simply through authority pressure, it would force a fundamental rethinking of moral responsibility, institutional design, and the power structures embedded in everyday life.

Participants were told they were taking part in a learning study. They were assigned the role of "teacher" and instructed by a researcher in a lab coat to administer electric shocks to a "learner" (actually an actor) every time he gave a wrong answer, increasing the voltage with each mistake. The shock generator was labelled from 15 volts ("Slight Shock") to 450 volts ("XXX"). As the experiment progressed, the learner screamed in pain, begged to stop, and eventually fell silent. Despite visible distress — sweating, trembling, nervous laughter — 65% of participants continued all the way to the maximum 450-volt shock when the experimenter calmly insisted they must continue. They did not enjoy it. Many protested verbally. But when the authority figure took responsibility and repeated scripted prompts like "the experiment requires that you continue," the majority complied.

For consumer behaviour and marketing, Milgram's findings illuminate the power of perceived authority in shaping compliance. Brands routinely leverage authority cues — white coats in adverts, expert endorsements, official-sounding language — to drive consumer action. The study also explains why unethical corporate cultures persist: employees follow directives from authority figures even when they privately disagree, especially when responsibility feels diffused across a chain of command. More broadly, it warns that the same principles making authority-based marketing effective can manipulate people into decisions that harm their own interests, making ethical guardrails in advertising and institutional design essential.
