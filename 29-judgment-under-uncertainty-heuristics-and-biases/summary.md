# Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases (1974) — Tversky & Kahneman

When people face uncertain situations — estimating probabilities, predicting outcomes, making judgments with incomplete information — how do they actually think? In 1974, Amos Tversky and Daniel Kahneman published one of the most influential papers in the history of psychology, arguing that people do not calculate probabilities rationally. Instead, they rely on a small number of mental shortcuts called heuristics. Understanding these shortcuts mattered because they are not just occasional quirks — they are the default operating system of human judgment, used by everyone from doctors diagnosing patients to investors pricing stocks to jurors weighing evidence.

Tversky and Kahneman identified three core heuristics. Representativeness leads people to judge probability by how closely something matches a mental prototype — so a quiet, bookish person is judged more likely to be a librarian than a salesperson, even when salespeople vastly outnumber librarians. Availability causes people to estimate the frequency of events based on how easily examples come to mind — so deaths from dramatic causes like plane crashes feel more common than deaths from mundane causes like diabetes, because crashes dominate news coverage. Anchoring occurs when people make estimates by starting from an initial value and adjusting insufficiently — so asking "Is the population of Turkey greater or less than 10 million?" produces systematically lower estimates than asking "greater or less than 100 million?", even though the anchor is arbitrary. Each heuristic is useful most of the time but produces systematic, predictable errors in specific situations.

For marketing, policy, and consumer behaviour, this paper laid the intellectual foundation for decades of applied behavioural science. Anchoring explains why showing a high "original price" next to a sale price makes discounts feel larger. Availability explains why brands invest heavily in memorable advertising — products that come to mind easily are judged as more popular and trustworthy. Representativeness explains why packaging and branding that match consumer stereotypes of quality outperform objectively superior alternatives that look unfamiliar. Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated that human irrationality is not random noise — it follows rules that can be mapped, predicted, and, inevitably, exploited.
