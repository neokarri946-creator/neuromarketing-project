# Influence: Science and Practice (1984) — Cialdini

Robert Cialdini spent years going undercover — training as a car salesman, joining telemarketing firms, and sitting through high-pressure sales pitches — to answer one question: why do people say yes? His research identified six universal principles of persuasion that compliance professionals exploit, often without their targets realising it. This work mattered because it moved the study of influence from abstract theory into the real world, documenting the specific psychological shortcuts that make humans predictably susceptible to manipulation. For the first time, both practitioners and consumers had a clear map of how persuasion actually works in everyday life.

The six principles Cialdini identified are reciprocity (we feel obligated to return favours, even uninvited ones), commitment and consistency (once we take a stand or make a choice, we feel internal pressure to behave consistently with that commitment), social proof (when uncertain, we look to what others are doing to determine correct behaviour), authority (we defer to perceived experts and authority figures), liking (we are more easily persuaded by people we find attractive, similar to us, or who pay us compliments), and scarcity (opportunities seem more valuable when they appear limited or diminishing). Each principle operates as a mental shortcut — a heuristic that usually serves us well but can be weaponised. Cialdini showed through extensive field research and controlled experiments that these six levers account for the vast majority of successful influence attempts across cultures and contexts.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, Cialdini's framework became foundational. Free samples exploit reciprocity. "Join 10 million satisfied customers" leverages social proof. Limited-edition releases manufacture scarcity. Celebrity endorsements combine authority and liking. Trial subscriptions that auto-renew exploit commitment and consistency. The power of this framework is not just that it explains existing tactics, but that it predicts which new ones will work. Any marketing strategy that activates one or more of these principles gains a measurable advantage, and strategies that accidentally violate them — such as admitting abundant stock or using unlikeable spokespeople — carry a predictable cost.
