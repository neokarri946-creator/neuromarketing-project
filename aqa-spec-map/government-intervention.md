## How Governments Use Behavioural Economics

### 1. Opt-Out Organ Donation

Instead of asking people to sign up to be an organ donor (opt-in), some countries assume everyone is a donor unless they say otherwise (opt-out).

**Why it works:** Most people never change the default. In opt-in countries like the UK (before 2020), donation rates were around 15-20%. In opt-out countries like Austria, rates were above 99%.

**Studies:** Study 30 (Do Defaults Save Lives?), Study 40 (Defaults and Donation Decisions)

### 2. Auto-Enrolment Pensions

Since 2012, UK employers automatically enrol workers into a pension scheme. You can opt out, but most people don't.

**Why it works:** Bounded self-control means people know they should save for retirement but keep putting it off. Making it the default removes the need for willpower.

**Studies:** Study 31 (401(k) Participation), Study 38 (Save More Tomorrow)

### 3. Sugar Tax Framing

The UK soft drinks industry levy (2018) was partly about revenue, but also about framing. By labelling it a "sugar tax", the government signalled that sugary drinks are unhealthy — creating a social norm effect alongside the price effect.

### 4. Calorie Labelling

Requiring restaurants and cafes to display calorie counts is a nudge. It doesn't ban unhealthy food — it just makes the information visible at the point of decision.

**Why it works:** It combats bounded rationality. People often underestimate how many calories they're consuming. Putting the number right there helps them make more informed choices.

### 5. The Behavioural Insights Team (UK "Nudge Unit")

Set up in 2010 inside the UK government. Their job is to apply behavioural science to public policy.

**Some of their wins:**
- Adding a single line to tax letters — "Most people in your area have already paid their tax" — increased payment rates by 15%
- Simplifying the process for people to sign up for job training
- Redesigning organ donor registration prompts on the DVLA website

This is a real-world example of a government using behavioural economics systematically, not just as a one-off.

## Evaluation: Nudges vs Traditional Intervention

### Strengths of Nudge-Based Policy

- **Preserves freedom of choice** — nobody is forced to do anything
- **Low cost** — changing a default or rewriting a letter is far cheaper than enforcing a new law
- **Works with human psychology** — instead of fighting against how people think, it uses those patterns
- **Politically easier** — less resistance than bans or taxes
- **Can complement other policies** — nudges can be used alongside regulation, not instead of it

### Weaknesses of Nudge-Based Policy

- **May not be strong enough** for serious problems (addiction, pollution, poverty)
- **Can be manipulated by firms** — if governments can nudge, so can businesses, and businesses may nudge people toward harmful choices
- **Raises ethical questions** — is it right for the government to "steer" people, even gently?
- **Doesn't address root causes** — nudging people to eat less sugar doesn't fix food deserts, poverty, or lack of education
- **Effect may fade over time** — people might learn to ignore nudges
- **Harder to measure** — the impact of a nudge is harder to quantify than the impact of a tax

### When Traditional Intervention is Better

| Situation | Better approach |
|-----------|----------------|
| Serious harm (smoking, pollution) | Regulation, taxation, bans |
| Firms actively exploiting consumers | Legal protection, enforcement |
| Deep inequality or poverty | Redistribution, public spending |
| Urgent action needed | Legislation (nudges work slowly) |

## Exam Tip: How to Evaluate Nudges in a 25-Mark Answer

For a top-band 25-mark answer, you need to **compare and evaluate** different types of intervention. Here's a structure that works:

**Paragraph 1 — Explain the traditional approach:**
> "One approach to reducing [problem] is [tax/regulation/ban]. This would work by [mechanism]. For example, [real example]."

**Paragraph 2 — Introduce the nudge alternative:**
> "However, behavioural economists such as Thaler and Sunstein argue that a nudge-based approach may be more effective. Rather than restricting choice, the government could [specific nudge]. This is consistent with libertarian paternalism — guiding people toward better outcomes while preserving freedom of choice."

**Paragraph 3 — Evaluate the nudge:**
> "A limitation of this approach is that nudges may be insufficient when [reason]. Furthermore, [second weakness]. This suggests that while nudges can complement traditional intervention, they may not be a substitute for it."

**Final evaluation:**
> "On balance, the most effective policy response is likely to combine both approaches — using nudges to influence everyday decisions while retaining regulation and taxation for more serious market failures."

This structure shows the examiner you can analyse, evaluate, and make a supported judgement — which is exactly what the top mark band requires.
