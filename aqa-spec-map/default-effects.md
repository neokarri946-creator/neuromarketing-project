# Default Choices and Their Impact on Decision-Making

## AQA Spec Reference

Paper 1 — Section 3.1.4.8: Behavioural economics

The spec says students should understand how **default options** influence people's choices. Defaults are one of the most powerful nudges — and one of the easiest to explain and apply in the exam.

---

## Why Defaults Are So Powerful

There are **three main reasons** people tend to stick with whatever the default option is:

### 1. Effort

Changing the default requires action — filling in a form, making a phone call, navigating a website. Even a small amount of effort is enough to stop most people from switching.

Think of it like a gym membership. Signing up takes effort. But once you're signed up, **cancelling** also takes effort. So you keep paying even when you've stopped going.

### 2. Implied Endorsement

People assume the default is the **recommended** option. "If this is what they've pre-selected, it must be the best one — or at least a sensible one."

It's like going to a restaurant and ordering the chef's recommendation. You trust that whoever set the default knows what they're doing.

### 3. Constructed Preferences

Often, we don't actually have a strong preference. We think we do, but really we're building our preference on the spot based on what's in front of us.

If you don't have a strong opinion about something, the default fills the gap. It becomes your preference because it was already there.

## Real-World Examples

### Organ Donation — Opt-In vs Opt-Out

In countries where you have to **sign up** to be a donor (opt-in), donation rates are low — often 15-20%.

In countries where you **are** a donor unless you opt out, rates jump to over 90%.

Same people. Same decision. Different default. Dramatically different outcome.

**Studies:** Study 30 (Do Defaults Save Lives?), Study 40 (Defaults and Donation Decisions)

### Pension Auto-Enrolment

Before auto-enrolment, many UK workers didn't save into a pension — not because they didn't want to, but because signing up felt like too much hassle.

When the default changed to "you're in unless you opt out", participation rates jumped from around 60% to over 90%.

**Studies:** Study 31 (401(k) Participation)

### Cookie Consent Defaults

Most websites default to "accept all cookies." The button is big, colourful, and easy to click. "Manage preferences" is usually small, grey, and requires multiple clicks.

This is choice architecture in action — the default is designed to get the outcome the website wants.

### Phone Pre-Installed Apps

Your phone comes loaded with apps you didn't choose. Most people never delete them. Apple Maps, Samsung Health, Google Drive — they're there by default, so they stay.

This is why being the default app on a phone is worth billions of dollars to tech companies.

## Which Studies to Read

| Study | Why it's relevant |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Study 30** — Do Defaults Save Lives? (Johnson & Goldstein) | The classic organ donation study — clear evidence that defaults change life-or-death decisions |
| **Study 31** — 401(k) Participation (Madrian & Shea) | Shows default effects in pension savings — auto-enrolment massively increases participation |
| **Study 32** — Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser) | The psychological explanation for why defaults work — people prefer to stick with what's already in place |
| **Study 40** — Defaults and Donation Decisions | Experimental evidence showing that defaults shape charitable giving behaviour |

## Which Case Studies to Look At

- **Netflix** — Autoplay is on by default. "Continue watching" appears automatically. Subtitles, video quality, and profile settings all have defaults designed to maximise viewing time. You CAN change them. Most people don't.
- **Uber** — Default tip options (15%, 20%, 25%) are pre-set. The middle option is highlighted. The design nudges you toward tipping a specific amount rather than choosing your own.

## Exam Tip

Defaults are one of the **simplest and most effective** examples to use in an exam. They work in almost any question about consumer behaviour, government policy, or market failure.

A clean way to use them:

> "One of the most effective nudges is the use of default options. Research by Johnson and Goldstein (2003) found that organ donation rates were dramatically higher in countries with an opt-out system compared to opt-in. This demonstrates that consumer behaviour is heavily influenced by how choices are structured — challenging the assumption that individuals actively and rationally choose based on their true preferences."

**Key phrase to remember:** "The same choice, framed differently, produces a different outcome." This is the heart of why defaults matter in economics.
