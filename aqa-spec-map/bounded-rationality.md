# Bounded Rationality, Bounded Self-Control, Bounded Selfishness

## AQA Spec Reference

Paper 1 — Section 3.1.4.8: Behavioural economics

The spec says students should understand how people **don't always behave rationally** — and that there are three specific ways human decision-making falls short of the "perfectly rational" model that traditional economics assumes.

---

## Key Concepts

### Bounded Rationality

**In plain English:** We don't have perfect information, and even if we did, our brains can't process it all. So we take shortcuts.

Think of it like shopping in a massive supermarket with 50,000 products. You can't compare every single item, read every label, and calculate the best deal on everything. So you grab the brand you recognise, or the one on the shelf at eye level.

These mental shortcuts are called **heuristics** — they're useful most of the time, but they can lead us to make decisions that aren't actually the best for us.

Traditional economics says: "People weigh up all the options and pick the best one."
Behavioural economics says: "People do the best they can with limited time, limited information, and limited brainpower."

### Bounded Self-Control

**In plain English:** We know what's good for us, but we can't always resist temptation.

You know you should save money, but you buy the trainers anyway. You know you should revise, but you open TikTok instead.

It's the gap between what we **plan** to do and what we **actually** do. Traditional economics assumes people always follow through on their plans. Behavioural economics says: "Not so fast."

This is closely linked to **present bias** — we overvalue rewards we can have right now and undervalue rewards in the future.

### Bounded Selfishness

**In plain English:** We're not as selfish as traditional economics assumes. Sometimes we act fairly, even when it costs us.

If someone offers you an unfair deal, you might reject it — even though accepting would leave you better off. You'd rather get nothing than let someone take advantage of you.

People tip in restaurants they'll never visit again. People donate to charity. People share things when they don't have to.

Traditional economics says: "People always maximise their own gain."
Behavioural economics says: "People also care about fairness, reciprocity, and social norms."

## Which Studies to Read

| Study | Why it's relevant |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Study 29** — Heuristics and Biases (Tversky & Kahneman) | The foundational research on mental shortcuts — explains why bounded rationality leads to predictable errors |
| **Study 36** — Thinking, Fast and Slow (Kahneman) | System 1 (fast, automatic) vs System 2 (slow, deliberate) thinking — this IS bounded rationality in action |
| **Study 11** — Delayed Rewards (McClure et al.) | Brain scan evidence that different parts of the brain compete — one wants instant reward, the other wants to wait. This is bounded self-control happening in real time |
| **Study 20** — Predictably Irrational (Ariely) | Shows that our irrational behaviour isn't random — it follows patterns. We're not just bounded, we're predictably bounded |

## Which Case Studies to Look At

- **Betting companies** — A perfect example of bounded self-control being exploited. People know gambling is risky, but the instant thrill overrides their long-term judgement. The industry is designed to exploit this gap.
- **Amazon** — Exploits bounded rationality. Too many choices, too much information, so you rely on shortcuts like star ratings, "Amazon's Choice" badges, and "customers also bought" recommendations. Amazon knows you won't compare every option.

## Exam Tip

When answering a question about consumer behaviour or market failure, you can use bounded rationality to **challenge the assumption of rational choice**.

A strong sentence might look like:

> "Traditional economic theory assumes consumers are rational and will choose the option that maximises their utility. However, behavioural economists such as Kahneman argue that rationality is bounded — consumers use heuristics and mental shortcuts, which can lead to systematic errors in decision-making. This suggests that markets may not allocate resources as efficiently as the rational model predicts."

This works in **any** question about markets, consumer choice, or government intervention. It's a reliable evaluation point.
