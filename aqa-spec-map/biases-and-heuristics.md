# Cognitive Biases — Anchoring, Availability, Framing

## AQA Spec Reference

Paper 1 — Section 3.1.4.8: Behavioural economics

The spec says students should understand **cognitive biases** — systematic errors in thinking that lead people to make irrational decisions. You need to know the main types and be able to explain how they affect economic behaviour.

---

## The Three Biases You Need to Know

### 1. Anchoring

**In plain English:** The first number you see sticks in your head and pulls your judgement toward it — even if that number is completely irrelevant.

**How it works:**
If you see a jacket "was $120, now $60" — you feel like you're getting a bargain. But what if the jacket was never really worth $120? The "was" price is the anchor. It makes the $60 feel cheap by comparison.

**Real example — Amazon:**
Amazon shows you the "RRP" or "was" price crossed out next to the sale price. That crossed-out number is an anchor. It makes you feel the current price is a deal, even if the product was never actually sold at the higher price.

### 2. Availability Bias

**In plain English:** If you can easily think of examples of something, you assume it's more common or more likely than it really is.

**How it works:**
Your brain judges how likely something is based on how easily an example pops into your mind. Things that are dramatic, recent, or emotional are easier to recall — so we overestimate them.

**Real example — Fear of flying:**
More people are afraid of flying than driving, even though driving is statistically far more dangerous. Why? Plane crashes are dramatic, they're all over the news, you can picture them vividly. Car crashes happen every day but don't make headlines. So your brain thinks planes = more dangerous.

### 3. Framing

**In plain English:** The way information is presented changes the decision you make — even when the actual facts are identical.

**How it works:**
"90% fat free" and "contains 10% fat" are the exact same thing. But the first one sounds healthier. The frame changes how you feel about the information.

**Real example — Kahneman's Asian Disease Problem:**
Participants were told about a disease that would kill 600 people. They were given two options:

- **Frame A:** "200 people will be saved" — most people chose this
- **Frame B:** "There is a 1/3 chance that 600 will be saved, and a 2/3 chance nobody will be saved" — most people avoided this

Both options have the same expected outcome. But the way they're framed changes the choice. When framed as a gain ("saved"), people play it safe. When framed as a loss, people take risks.

## Which Studies to Read

| Study | Why it's relevant |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Study 29** — Heuristics and Biases (Tversky & Kahneman) | The original research paper that identified anchoring, availability, and other heuristics |
| **Study 09** — Choices, Values and Frames (Kahneman & Tversky) | Deep dive into framing effects and how the presentation of choices changes decisions |
| **Study 20** — Predictably Irrational (Ariely) | Shows how biases play out in everyday consumer behaviour — with accessible, real-world examples |
| **Study 08** — Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky) | The theory behind framing — explains why losses feel roughly twice as painful as equivalent gains feel good |

## Which Case Studies to Look At

- **Amazon** — Uses anchoring (crossed-out prices, "was/now"), availability (bestseller lists, trending items), and framing ("save 40%") constantly
- **Apple** — Anchors with the most expensive product first (e.g. iPhone Pro Max), making the standard model feel "reasonable" by comparison
- **Supermarkets** — Use framing everywhere: "3 for 2", "only $1.50 each when you buy 4", shelf positioning to exploit availability

## Exam Tip

Biases are brilliant for **explaining why markets fail**. The traditional model assumes consumers have perfect information and process it rationally. Biases show that even when information IS available, people process it badly.

A strong analytical point:

> "Anchoring bias means consumers may not respond to price signals as traditional theory predicts. If a firm sets an artificially high reference price, consumers may perceive a lower price as good value even if it exceeds the competitive equilibrium price. This represents an information failure and a source of market inefficiency."

Use biases to challenge the rational consumer model in **any** question about demand, consumer choice, or market failure.
