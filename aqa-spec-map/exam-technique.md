# Exam Technique — Using Behavioural Economics in Answers

## Paper 1: Markets Questions

Behavioural economics fits naturally into any Paper 1 question about consumer behaviour, market failure, or government intervention.

**How to introduce it:** After explaining the traditional economic argument, use BE as evaluation.

> "However, this analysis assumes that consumers behave rationally. Behavioural economists such as Kahneman and Thaler argue that decision-making is subject to cognitive biases and bounded rationality, which means..."

This works for questions on demand, price elasticity, merit/demerit goods, information failures, and government intervention.

## Paper 3: Synoptic Essays

Paper 3 rewards you for making connections across the spec. Behavioural economics is perfect for this because it links to almost everything.

**Use it to:**
- Challenge rational consumer assumptions in micro questions
- Evaluate government policies in macro questions (e.g. nudges vs taxation)
- Discuss why markets fail even when information is available
- Explain why government intervention doesn't always work (people don't always respond as expected)

## Key Evaluation Phrases

Keep these in your back pocket. They show the examiner you can think critically:

- "However, nudges may not work when the behaviour is driven by addiction or deep-rooted habits."
- "A limitation of behavioural economics is that it focuses on individual decision-making and may underestimate structural factors such as income inequality."
- "While nudges preserve consumer choice, critics argue they can be paternalistic and may not address the root causes of market failure."
- "Firms may use the same behavioural insights to exploit consumers rather than help them, which suggests regulation may still be necessary."
- "The effectiveness of nudges tends to diminish over time as people become aware of them."

## Linking BE to Core Topics

| Core topic | How BE connects |
|------------|----------------|
| **Market failure** | Biases cause information failure — people misprocess information even when it's available |
| **Government failure** | Nudges might be too weak; governments might misjudge what people need |
| **Rational decision-making** | BE directly challenges this assumption — use it as evaluation in any question that assumes rationality |
| **Merit/demerit goods** | People under-consume merit goods and over-consume demerit goods partly because of present bias and bounded self-control |

## Common Mistakes Students Make

- **Using BE without a specific example.** Don't just say "people are irrational." Name the bias, cite a study, give a real-world case.
- **Treating nudges as a magic fix.** Always evaluate — nudges have limits. The examiner wants to see you weigh both sides.
- **Forgetting to link back to the question.** BE is a tool, not the answer itself. Always connect it back to the specific market, policy, or scenario in the question.
- **Only using BE in behavioural economics questions.** The biggest missed opportunity. Use it in market failure, government intervention, and synoptic essays too.

This suggests that..."

**Starter 2 — Introducing nudges as policy:**
> "Rather than relying on traditional market-based interventions such as taxation, the government could adopt a nudge-based approach. Thaler and Sunstein (2008) argue that by redesigning choice architecture — for example, changing the default option — policymakers can influence behaviour without restricting freedom of choice. Evidence from..."

**Starter 3 — Evaluating a nudge:**
> "While nudge-based policies have shown promise — for instance, auto-enrolment increased UK pension participation from 60% to over 90% — they may be insufficient as a standalone response to [the issue in the question]. Behavioural interventions work best when the decision is one-off and low-stakes, but may struggle to change deeply entrenched behaviours such as..."

Use these as templates. Swap in the specific topic, study, or example that fits the question you're answering.
