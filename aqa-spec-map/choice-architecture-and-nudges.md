# Choice Architecture, Nudge Theory, Libertarian Paternalism

## AQA Spec Reference

Paper 1 — Section 3.1.4.8: Behavioural economics

The spec says students should understand **nudge theory** and how the design of choices influences decisions. You need to know what choice architecture means, how nudges work, and be able to evaluate whether they're an effective form of intervention.

---

## Key Concepts

### Choice Architecture

**In plain English:** The way options are arranged affects which one people pick.

Think of a school cafeteria. If healthy food is placed at eye level and junk food is hidden at the back, more students eat healthy food — even though all the same options are available. Nobody banned anything. The **design of the environment** did the work.

A choice architect is anyone who designs how options are presented — a website designer, a shop layout manager, a government form writer. Whether they know it or not, they're shaping decisions.

### Nudge

**In plain English:** A gentle push toward a better choice, without taking away your freedom to choose something else.

A nudge is NOT a ban. It's NOT a tax. It's NOT a fine. It's a small change in how choices are presented that makes people more likely to pick a particular option.

**Examples of nudges:**
- Putting fruit at the checkout instead of chocolate
- Making organ donation the default (you have to opt out, not opt in)
- Sending a text reminder before a doctor's appointment

The key thing: you can always ignore a nudge. Your freedom is intact.

### Libertarian Paternalism

**In plain English:** Guiding people toward better choices while keeping their freedom to choose.

This sounds like a contradiction — "libertarian" means freedom, "paternalism" means someone deciding what's best for you. But the idea is:

- **Paternalism:** We think we know which option is better for you
- **Libertarian:** But we'll never force you — you can always choose differently

It's the philosophy behind nudge theory. Help people make better decisions, but don't remove their options.

### Types of Nudges

| Type | What it means | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Defaults** | Pre-selecting an option so people stick with it | Pension auto-enrolment — you're in unless you opt out |
| **Framing** | Presenting the same info in a way that encourages a certain choice | "9 out of 10 dentists recommend" vs "1 in 10 dentists don't recommend" |
| **Social norms** | Telling people what others do | "8 out of 10 of your neighbours pay their tax on time" |
| **Simplification** | Making the better option easier | One-click donation buttons, pre-filled forms |

## Which Studies to Read

| Study | Why it's relevant |
|-------|-------------------|
| **Study 23** — Nudge (Thaler & Sunstein) | The book that defined nudge theory — covers choice architecture, defaults, and libertarian paternalism |
| **Study 30** — Do Defaults Save Lives? (Johnson & Goldstein) | Shows how switching from opt-in to opt-out organ donation dramatically increases donation rates |
| **Study 31** — 401(k) Participation (Madrian & Shea) | Shows how auto-enrolling people into pension savings massively increased participation — the power of defaults |
| **Study 32** — Status Quo Bias (Samuelson & Zeckhauser) | Explains WHY defaults work — people have a strong tendency to stick with whatever the current option is |

## Which Case Studies to Look At

- **Netflix** — Uses defaults constantly. Autoplay is on by default. The "continue watching" row is at the top. The default profile settings are designed to keep you watching. You CAN change them, but most people don't
- **Supermarkets** — Shelf placement is choice architecture. Eye-level products sell more. End-of-aisle displays nudge impulse buys. The layout is designed to guide your path through the store
- **Tesco (Clubcard)** — The Clubcard structure nudges you to shop at Tesco more often. Default voucher settings, personalised offers, and the points system all make Tesco the easy default choice

## Exam Tip

Nudge theory is one of the **highest-value evaluation tools** in your toolkit. It lets you compare behavioural interventions with traditional ones (taxes, bans, regulation).

When evaluating government policy, try this structure:

> "An alternative to [tax/regulation/ban] would be a nudge-based approach. For example, [specific nudge]. This preserves consumer choice — consistent with libertarian paternalism — and avoids the government failure risks associated with direct intervention. However, nudges may be insufficient when the behaviour is deeply ingrained or when firms have strong financial incentives to counter-nudge consumers."

This shows the examiner you can **compare** types of intervention, which is what gets you into the top mark bands.
