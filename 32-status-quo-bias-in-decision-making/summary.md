# Status Quo Bias in Decision Making (1988) — Samuelson & Zeckhauser

Economists had long assumed that rational decision-makers evaluate options on their merits, choosing whichever delivers the greatest expected value regardless of their starting position. William Samuelson and Richard Zeckhauser suspected this assumption was wrong. They designed a series of experiments to test whether people exhibit a systematic preference for the current state of affairs — not because it is objectively better, but simply because it is the status quo. Understanding this bias mattered because it could explain puzzling patterns across economics, politics, and everyday life where people clung to inferior options despite clear evidence that switching would benefit them.

Across multiple experiments, participants were given decision scenarios involving financial investments, job choices, and policy options. The critical manipulation was elegant: the same set of alternatives was presented either neutrally (with no option framed as the current situation) or with one option designated as the status quo. When an option was labelled as the existing state of affairs, participants chose it significantly more often than when it was presented as just another alternative. The bias grew stronger as the number of alternatives increased and as the decision became more complex. Even when the researchers made an alternative objectively superior by giving it better payoffs, the status quo option still commanded a disproportionate share of choices. Participants were not indifferent between equivalent options — they were actively anchored to whatever they already had.

This study provided the foundational framework for understanding why consumers resist switching brands, why voters re-elect incumbents, and why employees rarely change their default benefit selections. For marketers, it means that the first brand a consumer adopts enjoys a structural advantage that competitors must overcome with substantially better offerings, not merely equivalent ones. It explains why free trials convert so effectively — once a product becomes the status quo, cancelling feels like a loss. The bias also warns against unnecessary product redesigns, since customers may reject objectively better versions simply because they disrupt the familiar.
