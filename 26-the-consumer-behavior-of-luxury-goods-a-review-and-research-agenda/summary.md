# The Consumer Behavior of Luxury Goods: A Review and Research Agenda (2020) — Dhaliwal et al.

Why do people pay hundreds or thousands of pounds for a handbag, a watch, or a pair of shoes when functionally equivalent alternatives exist at a fraction of the cost? Dhaliwal et al. set out to synthesise decades of luxury consumption research into a coherent framework, because the luxury market — worth over $300 billion globally — had been studied in fragments but never mapped as a whole system. Understanding what actually drives luxury purchases matters enormously: luxury brands shape aspirations, signal social position, and increasingly influence mainstream consumer culture as "masstige" (mass-market prestige) products blur the boundaries.

The review identified three interlocking categories of influence on luxury buying. Personal factors include self-esteem, the need for uniqueness, hedonic pleasure-seeking, and the desire to signal achievement to oneself. Social factors encompass reference group pressure, cultural norms around status display, conspicuous consumption in collectivist versus individualist societies, and the role of social media in amplifying visibility. Brand-level factors involve heritage and craftsmanship narratives, perceived exclusivity, sensory aesthetics, and the careful management of scarcity. Critically, the authors found that these three categories do not operate in isolation — they interact dynamically. For instance, a consumer's need for uniqueness (personal) is shaped by their cultural context (social) and satisfied only when the brand credibly delivers exclusivity (brand). The review also flagged two forces reshaping luxury: digital channels, which threaten exclusivity by increasing accessibility, and rising sustainability expectations, which challenge the traditional luxury emphasis on rare materials and excess.

For marketers and brand strategists, the framework offers a practical diagnostic. Luxury brands must simultaneously manage three tensions: exclusivity versus digital reach, heritage versus innovation, and indulgence versus ethical responsibility. Brands that lean too far toward accessibility erode the scarcity signal that justifies premium pricing. Those that ignore digital and sustainability risk irrelevance among younger consumers who expect transparency and purpose. The research agenda highlights that the most resilient luxury brands will be those that integrate personal meaning, social signalling, and authentic brand narratives into a coherent identity — rather than relying on any single driver alone.
