# Marketing Actions Can Modulate Neural Representations of Experienced Pleasantness (2008) — Plassmann et al.

Can the price tag on a bottle of wine literally change how good it tastes — not just what you say about it, but how your brain actually experiences the flavour? Hilke Plassmann and colleagues at the California Institute of Technology set out to test whether marketing information like price could alter the neural encoding of pleasure itself. This was a crucial question because it challenged the fundamental economic assumption that experienced quality is objective and fixed. If price could change the actual neural experience, it would mean marketing does not merely influence decisions — it rewrites sensory reality.

The researchers placed participants in an fMRI scanner and gave them sips of wine labelled with different prices. The critical manipulation was that some wines were identical liquids presented at different price points — for example, the same wine labelled as both £5 and £45. Participants consistently rated the "expensive" wine as more pleasant. But the striking finding was neural, not behavioural: the medial orbitofrontal cortex (mOFC), a brain region that encodes experienced pleasantness, showed significantly greater activation for wines believed to be expensive. This was not participants trying to seem sophisticated. Their brains were generating a genuinely different and more pleasurable experience in response to the higher price. The primary taste cortex, which processes raw sensory input, showed no difference — the tongue tasted the same thing, but the brain constructed a different experience.

This study has profound implications for marketing strategy and consumer behaviour. It demonstrates that pricing is not merely a signal of quality — it is an active ingredient in the consumption experience. Premium pricing, luxury branding, and elegant packaging do not just set expectations; they physically enhance the pleasure consumers derive from products. This helps explain why store-brand products struggle even when they win blind taste tests, why luxury brands fiercely protect their price floors, and why discounting can paradoxically damage a product's perceived quality even when the product itself is unchanged.
