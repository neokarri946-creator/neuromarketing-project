# The Psychology of Luxury Consumption (2020) — Dubois, Jung & Ordabayeva

Why do people spend thousands on a handbag when a cheaper one carries the same items? The answer seems obvious — status — but David Dubois, Seonghee Jung, and Nailya Ordabayeva argued that reducing luxury to status-signalling misses most of the psychology. Their 2020 review synthesised decades of research to ask a more precise question: what are the distinct psychological motives behind luxury purchases, how do they differ across people and cultures, and when does each motive dominate? Understanding this mattered because the global luxury market exceeded $300 billion, and brands that misidentified why their customers bought would inevitably misfire with messaging, pricing, and product design.

The authors identified four primary psychological drivers of luxury consumption. First, status signalling — using visible luxury goods to communicate wealth and social rank to others. Second, self-reward — purchasing luxury as a private treat to celebrate achievements or soothe negative emotions, with no audience required. Third, uniqueness-seeking — choosing rare or limited items to feel distinct from the crowd and express individuality. Fourth, conformity to aspirational groups — buying what admired social circles buy in order to gain acceptance or feel belonging. These motives are not fixed: they shift depending on culture (collectivist societies emphasise conformity more, individualist cultures emphasise uniqueness), personality (narcissism amplifies status motives), social context (public versus private consumption), and emotional state (guilt suppresses luxury consumption, while feelings of power enhance it). The review showed that the same product can be purchased for entirely different psychological reasons by different consumers.

For luxury marketers, the practical takeaway is segmentation by motive rather than by demographics. A customer buying a Rolex for status responds to exclusivity cues and visible branding, while one buying it as self-reward responds to craftsmanship and personal meaning. Brands that recognise these distinct pathways can tailor advertising, retail experience, and even product design to match the underlying motive — and avoid the common mistake of assuming every luxury buyer wants the same thing. The framework also explains why some luxury brands succeed by being loud and logo-heavy while others thrive on quiet, understated design: they are serving different psychological needs.
