# Predictably Irrational (2008) — Ariely

Traditional economics assumed that people are rational agents who weigh costs and benefits to maximise their own utility. Dan Ariely, a behavioural economist at Duke University, spent years running experiments that systematically dismantled this assumption. In his 2008 book, he synthesised this research around a central argument: people are not just irrational — they are irrational in consistent, repeatable, predictable ways. This distinction mattered enormously, because if irrationality is random it cannot be studied or anticipated, but if it follows patterns it becomes a science with practical applications for marketing, policy, and business design.

Ariely demonstrated several key distortions. The decoy effect showed that adding a clearly inferior third option changes how people choose between the original two — for example, offering a print-only subscription alongside a print-and-digital bundle made the bundle look irresistibly valuable, even though people ignored the print-only option. The power of "free" revealed that zero price is not just a low price but a qualitatively different psychological category: people will choose a free item over a better deal if it involves any cost at all, because "free" eliminates the fear of loss. Anchoring showed that arbitrary initial numbers — even obviously random ones — dragged subsequent valuations toward them. And the distinction between social and market norms demonstrated that introducing money into social relationships destroys goodwill in ways that cannot easily be reversed: paying your mother-in-law for a home-cooked dinner insults rather than rewards her.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, Ariely's work provided a toolkit of predictable levers. The decoy effect is now standard in subscription pricing, where a strategically inferior middle tier steers customers toward the most profitable option. Free shipping thresholds exploit the irrational magnetism of "free" to increase basket sizes. Anchoring is built into every sale that shows the "original price" before the discount. These are not tricks that exploit ignorance — they are systematic features of human cognition that shape every purchasing decision, and understanding them gives marketers the ability to design choice environments that guide behaviour without restricting freedom.
