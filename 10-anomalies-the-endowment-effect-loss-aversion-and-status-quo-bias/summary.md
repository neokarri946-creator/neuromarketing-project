# Anomalies: The Endowment Effect, Loss Aversion, and Status Quo Bias (1991) — Kahneman, Knetsch & Thaler

Standard economic theory held that a person's willingness to pay for an item and their willingness to accept payment to give it up should be roughly equal — if a mug is worth $5 to you, you should buy and sell it at around that price. Kahneman, Knetsch, and Thaler documented three related anomalies that shatter this assumption: the endowment effect (people value things more once they own them), loss aversion (losses are felt more acutely than equivalent gains), and status quo bias (people prefer to keep things as they are). These interconnected biases distort markets, policy decisions, and everyday consumer behaviour in systematic, predictable ways.

The authors drew on extensive experimental evidence. In the classic mug experiments, half the participants were randomly given coffee mugs and asked for a minimum selling price, while the other half were asked what they would pay to buy one. Sellers demanded roughly twice what buyers would pay — not because the mugs differed, but because owning the mug made it feel more valuable. The endowment effect, they argued, stems directly from loss aversion: giving up the mug is coded as a loss, and losses weigh more heavily than gains. Status quo bias operates through the same mechanism — switching from the current state means accepting the loss of what you have, which feels worse than the potential gain of something new, even when the new option is objectively better.

These three biases have enormous practical consequences. They explain why consumers stick with default phone plans, insurance policies, and subscription services long after better alternatives appear. They explain why free trial periods convert so effectively — once someone "owns" the product, cancelling feels like a loss. For marketers, the lesson is clear: get the product into the customer's hands first, and the endowment effect does the selling. For policymakers, the lesson is equally clear: defaults matter enormously, because most people will not switch away from whatever is pre-selected.
