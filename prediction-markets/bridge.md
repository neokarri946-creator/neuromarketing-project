# From Consumer Biases to Market Mispricing

## What Are Prediction Markets?

A prediction market is like a betting shop for real-world events.

People buy and sell contracts on things like "Will Labour win the next election?" or "Will Bitcoin hit $100K by December?"

The price of each contract reflects what the crowd thinks the probability is. If a contract trades at 70p, the crowd is saying: "We think there's a 70% chance this happens."

## Why They Should Be Accurate

In theory, prediction markets should be really good at forecasting.

- **Lots of people** are contributing their opinions (wisdom of crowds)
- **Real money** is at stake, so people think carefully
- **New information** gets priced in quickly as traders react

When you put thousands of people together, each with their own knowledge, the group average *should* be better than any individual guess.

## Why They're Not Always Accurate

Here's the catch: the crowd is made of humans. And humans have biases.

The same mental shortcuts that make someone impulse-buy a product they don't need also make someone overpay for a prediction market contract.

The brain doesn't switch to a different operating system when it goes from shopping to betting. It uses the same wiring. The same shortcuts. The same mistakes.

## The Core Insight

This is where your neuromarketing project connects directly to prediction markets:

**The 40 studies you've already analysed don't just explain why people buy things. They explain why prediction markets get things wrong.**

Every bias that distorts consumer behaviour also distorts how people price probabilities.

## A Quick Example

Take **availability bias** (Study 29). People overestimate the likelihood of dramatic, memorable events.

If there's just been a major terrorist attack in the news, the contract "Will there be another terrorist attack this month?" will get bid up on Polymarket -- not because the actual risk has changed much, but because the event is *vivid and available* in people's minds.

The contract is now overpriced. The crowd is wrong. And if you understand *why* they're wrong, you have an edge.

## What This Means

If you can identify which biases are distorting a market, you can:

- Spot contracts that are overpriced (the crowd is too scared or too excited)
- Spot contracts that are underpriced (the crowd is ignoring something boring but important)
- Bet against the crowd when you know their thinking is skewed

This isn't guessing. It's applied behavioural economics -- using what you already know about how the brain works.
