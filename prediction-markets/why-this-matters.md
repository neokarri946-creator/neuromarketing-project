# Why This Matters -- The Road to the Prediction Model

## What You've Built So Far

You started with a question: *How does the brain decide what to buy?*

That question turned into a serious body of work:

- **40 academic studies** analysed -- covering neuroscience, behavioural economics, and consumer psychology
- **10 case studies** showing how real companies exploit these biases
- **An AQA specification map** connecting everything back to your Economics syllabus
- **A bridge** (this folder) showing that the same biases affect prediction markets

Each step built on the last. Nothing was wasted.

## The Journey

"I started by understanding how the brain makes decisions. Then I saw how companies exploit that. Then I realised the same biases show up wherever humans make judgments under uncertainty -- including financial markets."

That's a powerful arc. Most people study consumer behaviour and financial markets as separate things. You've connected them.

## What the Prediction Model Would Do

The final project takes everything above and makes it practical.

The model would:

- **Scan Polymarket contracts** to identify active markets
- **Flag which cognitive biases** are likely distorting each contract's price
- **Estimate the direction of mispricing** -- is the contract overpriced or underpriced because of those biases?
- **Generate signals** -- a shortlist of contracts where the crowd is probably wrong, and why

Think of it as a bias detector for betting markets.

## Why This Is Valuable

Prediction markets are growing fast. Polymarket alone handled billions of dollars in volume during 2024, and the space is expanding.

Most participants are regular people -- amateurs with the same biases you've spent months studying. They bring real money but also real cognitive errors.

That's the opportunity.

## Your Edge

Most quantitative models look for patterns in **data** -- price movements, trading volumes, historical trends.

Your model would look for patterns in **psychology** -- which biases are active, how they distort prices, and where the crowd's thinking is systematically wrong.

That's a different and complementary approach. It doesn't compete with data-driven models. It adds a layer they're missing.

## The Intersection

This project sits at the crossroads of three fields:

- **Economics** -- market efficiency, pricing, and incentives
- **Psychology** -- cognitive biases, heuristics, and decision-making
- **Data science** -- building a model that turns theory into actionable predictions

Very few people work at that intersection. You're building toward it, one step at a time.
