# How Each Bias Distorts Prediction Markets

This maps 10 biases from the neuromarketing project to specific ways they cause mispricing on platforms like Polymarket.

---

## 1. Availability Heuristic

**What it is:** We judge how likely something is based on how easily we can think of an example.

**How it distorts markets:** Dramatic, recent, or media-heavy events get overpriced. Quiet, boring risks get underpriced.

**Polymarket example:** A major earthquake just hit Japan. The contract "Will there be a magnitude 7+ earthquake this month?" jumps from 40% to 70% -- even though the base rate hasn't changed.

**Study link:** Study 29 -- *Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases* (Tversky & Kahneman)

---

## 2. Anchoring

**What it is:** The first number we see sticks in our heads and pulls our estimates toward it, even if it's irrelevant.

**How it distorts markets:** Whatever price a contract opens at becomes an anchor. The market adjusts too slowly, even when new data comes in.

**Polymarket example:** "Will the UK enter a recession?" opens at 55%. Strong GDP data comes out, but the price only drifts to 48%. Without the anchor, it might be 30%.

**Study link:** Study 29 -- *Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases* (Tversky & Kahneman)

---

## 3. Optimism Bias

**What it is:** We believe good things are more likely to happen to us (and our side) than the evidence supports.

**How it distorts markets:** Partisan bettors overpay for their preferred outcome. They're not pricing probability -- they're pricing hope.

**Polymarket example:** Republican supporters pile into "Will the Republican win?" pushing it to 65%, while polls suggest 50/50. Betting with hearts, not heads.

**Study link:** Study 39 -- *Unrealistic Optimism About Future Life Events* (Weinstein)

---

## 4. Herd Behaviour / Social Conformity

**What it is:** We follow what others are doing, assuming they know something we don't.

**How it distorts markets:** Price momentum gets mistaken for real information. A few big trades can trigger a cascade where everyone piles in.

**Polymarket example:** A contract on "Will AI pass the bar exam by 2027?" moves from 30% to 55% in a day after a few large bets. Other traders see the movement and buy in, thinking "the smart money knows something." But it was just one whale. The price overshoots.

**Study link:** Studies 17 and 37 -- *Reinforcement of the Neural Valuation Signal by Social Conformity* (Klucharev et al.) and *Opinions and Social Pressure* (Asch)

---

## 5. Loss Aversion

**What it is:** Losing hurts about twice as much as winning feels good. So we avoid realising losses, even when we should.

**How it distorts markets:** People who bought a contract at 60% won't sell at 40%, even when the evidence says they should. They hold on, hoping it recovers. This keeps prices too high for too long.

**Polymarket example:** Bettors who bought "Will the ceasefire hold?" at 70% refuse to sell when it drops to 35% after hostilities resume. The price stays inflated because holders won't cut their losses.

**Study link:** Studies 08 and 10 -- *Prospect Theory* (Kahneman & Tversky) and *The Endowment Effect, Loss Aversion, and Status Quo Bias* (Thaler)

---

## 6. Framing Effects

**What it is:** How a question is worded changes how we answer it, even if the underlying facts are the same.

**How it distorts markets:** Two contracts about the same event can be priced inconsistently depending on how the question is framed.

**Polymarket example:** "Will inflation stay above 3%?" is priced at 60%. Logically, "Will inflation drop below 3%?" should be at 40%. But it's priced at 45%. The framing creates a 5% gap that shouldn't exist.

**Study link:** Study 09 -- *Choices, Values, and Frames* (Kahneman & Tversky)

---

## 7. Representativeness

**What it is:** We judge probabilities by how much something *looks like* a pattern we recognise, rather than using actual base rates.

**How it distorts markets:** People see surface-level similarities between situations and assume the same outcome will happen. Bad pattern matching.

**Polymarket example:** "This election feels just like 2016 -- the polls are wrong again!" Traders overweight the 2016 analogy and overprice the underdog, ignoring that polling methodology has improved since then.

**Study link:** Study 29 -- *Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases* (Tversky & Kahneman)

---

## 8. Status Quo Bias

**What it is:** We prefer things to stay the way they are. Change feels risky, even when it isn't.

**How it distorts markets:** "Things will stay the same" outcomes get overpriced. Incumbents, current trends, and continuity all get a premium they don't deserve.

**Polymarket example:** "Will the incumbent president win re-election?" is priced at 62%, even though historical data and current polling suggest 50/50. The market defaults to expecting continuity.

**Study link:** Study 32 -- *Status Quo Bias in Decision Making* (Samuelson & Zeckhauser)

---

## 9. Overconfidence / Dunning-Kruger

**What it is:** People who know a little about a topic think they know a lot. They overestimate their own judgment.

**How it distorts markets:** Amateur bettors trade too aggressively on weak analysis, adding noise to prices -- especially in smaller, less-traded markets.

**Polymarket example:** Someone reads one article about the Fed and bets heavily on "Will interest rates be cut this quarter?" Their confidence far exceeds their actual understanding. In thin markets, a few overconfident traders can move the price significantly.

**Study link:** Study 35 -- *Unskilled and Unaware of It* (Kruger & Dunning)

---

## 10. Recency Bias

**What it is:** We give too much weight to things that happened recently, and not enough to long-term patterns.

**How it distorts markets:** Recent performance gets treated as a permanent trend. Short-term noise drowns out long-term signal.

**Polymarket example:** A football team wins three matches in a row. "Will they win the league?" jumps from 15% to 35%. But three games is a tiny sample. The price overshoots because traders are overweighting what just happened.

**Study link:** Related to Study 29 -- *Judgment Under Uncertainty: Heuristics and Biases* (Tversky & Kahneman)
