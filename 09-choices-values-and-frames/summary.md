# Choices, Values, and Frames (1984) — Kahneman & Tversky

If two options produce exactly the same outcome, people should choose between them the same way regardless of how the choice is worded. Kahneman and Tversky set out to demonstrate that this seemingly obvious principle is routinely violated — that the frame surrounding a decision changes the decision itself. This mattered because rational choice theory, the foundation of economics and policy-making, depended on the assumption that people respond to substance rather than framing. If framing alone could flip preferences, then the way a government, doctor, or marketer presents options would be as consequential as the options themselves.

The most famous demonstration was the "Asian Disease Problem." Participants were told that 600 people would die from an outbreak and were offered two programmes. When framed as gains — Programme A saves 200 people; Programme B gives a one-third chance of saving all 600 and a two-thirds chance of saving nobody — 72% chose the certain option (A). When the identical outcomes were framed as losses — Programme C means 400 people die; Programme D gives a one-third chance that nobody dies and a two-thirds chance that all 600 die — 78% chose the gamble (D). The options in each pair are mathematically identical, yet framing as gains produced risk aversion while framing as losses produced risk seeking. Kahneman and Tversky extended this pattern across dozens of scenarios, showing it was robust, replicable, and deeply embedded in human cognition.

The practical implications reshaped marketing, medicine, and public policy. A surgeon who says "90% of patients survive this operation" gets more consent than one who says "10% of patients die" — same statistic, different frame, different decision. Marketers frame products as preventing loss ("don't miss this deal") rather than enabling gain because the loss frame triggers stronger emotional responses. For consumer behaviour, the study revealed that preferences are not stable properties of individuals — they are constructed in the moment, shaped by whoever controls the frame.
