# When Choice Is Demotivating: Can One Desire Too Much of a Good Thing? (2000) — Iyengar & Lepper

In a gourmet food store in Menlo Park, California, Sheena Iyengar and Mark Lepper set up one of the most famous experiments in consumer psychology. The question was simple but commercially vital: does offering more product varieties attract more buyers, or does it actually drive them away? At the time, the default assumption in retail was that extensive assortment was always better — more flavours, more colours, more models meant more chances to match what the customer wanted. Iyengar and Lepper suspected this logic had a breaking point.

On alternating days, the researchers set up a tasting booth displaying either 6 or 24 varieties of premium jam. The large display drew more shoppers to the table — 60% of passers-by stopped, compared to 40% for the small display. But what happened next was striking. Of those who sampled from the 24-jam display, only 3% went on to purchase. Of those who sampled from the 6-jam display, 30% bought a jar. The extensive assortment was ten times less effective at converting interest into action. Follow-up laboratory studies confirmed the pattern: participants choosing from large assortments reported enjoying the process less, found the decision more difficult, and were more likely to opt out entirely rather than commit to a choice. The large array attracted attention but undermined the motivation to act on it.

The jam study became a landmark in marketing because it provided clean experimental evidence that choice overload is real and commercially costly. It reshaped how retailers and product designers think about assortment strategy. Companies learned that curating a focused range — a "sweet spot" of variety — could increase both conversion rates and customer satisfaction. The findings influenced everything from how supermarkets organise shelves to how streaming services design recommendation pages. Iyengar and Lepper demonstrated that attention and action are not the same thing: capturing a customer's interest means nothing if the sheer volume of options prevents them from making a decision and walking away satisfied.
