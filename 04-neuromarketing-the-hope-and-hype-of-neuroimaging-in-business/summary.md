# Neuromarketing: The Hope and Hype of Neuroimaging in Business (2010) — Ariely & Berns

By 2010, neuromarketing had become a buzzword. Companies were claiming they could use brain scans to find a "buy button" in consumers' heads, and marketing firms were charging enormous fees for neuroimaging-based insights. Dan Ariely and Gregory Berns, both neuroscientists with expertise in decision-making, asked a critical question: what can neuroimaging actually deliver for business, and where does legitimate science end and overblown hype begin? Their review mattered because it came at a moment when commercial neuromarketing was growing rapidly but with little scrutiny of its scientific foundations.

Ariely and Berns identified two scenarios where neuroimaging could genuinely help marketers. First, it could be cheaper than traditional methods — for instance, a single fMRI study of 20 people might predict population-wide preferences more efficiently than surveying thousands. Second, it could reveal hidden information that people cannot or will not report, such as unconscious emotional reactions or preferences that consumers are unaware of or reluctant to admit. However, the authors also catalogued serious problems. fMRI has limited spatial and temporal resolution, making it difficult to pinpoint exactly what a consumer is feeling in real time. Many commercial claims were based on reverse inference — seeing activation in a brain region and assuming it means a specific emotion — which is scientifically unreliable because most brain regions serve multiple functions. They also raised ethical concerns about consumer manipulation and privacy.

The practical takeaway is one of calibrated scepticism. Neuromarketing has real potential when it reveals genuinely hidden preferences — such as predicting which advertisements will succeed at a population level or detecting unconscious brand associations that surveys miss. But much of what commercial neuromarketing firms sell is dressed-up conventional market research with a brain-scan veneer. For students and practitioners of consumer behaviour, this paper provides essential critical thinking tools: always ask whether the neuroimaging data reveals something surveys cannot, whether the interpretation follows sound methodology, and whether the claims match the actual capabilities of the technology.
