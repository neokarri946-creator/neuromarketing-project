# The Dopamine Economy: Neuro-Marketing and Digital Consumer Behaviour (2025) — Jain & Mishra

Every time a notification lights up your phone, your brain releases a small hit of dopamine — not because the notification is rewarding, but because it might be. Jain and Mishra explored how digital platforms have industrialised this neurochemical loop, designing interfaces that exploit the brain's reward prediction system to create compulsive engagement. This question was urgent because the scale of digital consumption had outpaced regulators' and consumers' understanding of why these platforms feel so addictive, and a neuroscientific explanation was needed to bridge the gap between platform design and compulsive behaviour.

The authors synthesised research from neuroscience, behavioural economics, and digital marketing to map how specific platform features — variable reward schedules, infinite scroll, push notifications, and personalised content feeds — map onto dopamine-driven reinforcement pathways. Variable rewards are the critical mechanism: just as a slot machine keeps players engaged because the next pull might pay out, social media feeds deliver unpredictable bursts of social validation (likes, comments, shares) that keep users scrolling. The unpredictability is essential — predictable rewards cause dopamine responses to flatline, while uncertain ones sustain elevated dopamine activity and compulsive checking. Notifications function as conditioned cues that trigger anticipatory dopamine even before the phone is unlocked, training the brain to associate the alert sound with potential reward.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, the implications are profound and double-edged. On one side, these dopamine mechanics explain why digital advertising achieves engagement rates that traditional media never could — users are neurochemically primed to keep interacting. On the other, the study raises serious ethical questions about persuasive design that bypasses conscious decision-making. For consumers, recognising that the urge to check your phone is a manufactured dopamine response — not genuine interest — is the first step toward regaining autonomy. For regulators and designers, the study argues that understanding neuromarketing is no longer optional: platforms are engineering behaviour at the neurochemical level.
