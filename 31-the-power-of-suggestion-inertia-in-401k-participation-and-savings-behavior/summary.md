# The Power of Suggestion: Inertia in 401(k) Participation and Savings Behavior (2001) — Madrian & Shea

When companies offer retirement savings plans, employees must typically fill out paperwork to enrol — a system known as opt-in. Brigitte Madrian and Dennis Shea asked whether this small administrative friction was silently costing millions of workers their financial futures. The question mattered enormously: retirement savings shortfalls were becoming a national crisis, yet economists largely assumed people made rational, deliberate choices about their money. If something as trivial as a form could determine whether someone saved for retirement, it would challenge the entire foundation of how policymakers thought about financial behaviour.

Madrian and Shea studied a large US corporation that switched its 401(k) plan from opt-in to automatic enrolment — meaning new employees were enrolled by default unless they actively chose to opt out. The results were dramatic. Under opt-in, only 49% of employees participated after three months. Under automatic enrolment, participation surged to 86%. But the effects went deeper than just participation rates. Employees overwhelmingly stuck with whatever default contribution rate and investment fund the company selected for them. Even years later, the majority of automatically enrolled workers had never changed their initial settings. The default didn't just nudge the first decision — it anchored the entire trajectory of people's retirement savings.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, this study became one of the most cited demonstrations of default power. It proved that people don't choose between options so much as they accept whatever is already chosen for them. This insight reshaped everything from subscription models to organ donation policies, software privacy settings to insurance plan design. Marketers learned that the option you pre-select is the option most people will keep — making default architecture one of the most powerful tools in behavioural design. The ethical implication is equally significant: whoever controls the default controls the outcome, which means default-setters carry an outsized responsibility for the welfare of the people they serve.
