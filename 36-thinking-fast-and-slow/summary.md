# Thinking, Fast and Slow (2011) — Kahneman

Daniel Kahneman's 2011 book synthesised decades of research into a single framework that reshaped how economists, marketers, and policymakers understand human judgment. The central question was deceptively broad: why do smart people make predictable mistakes? Kahneman argued that answering this required abandoning the assumption that human thinking is a single, unified process. Instead, he proposed that the mind operates through two distinct modes — and that the interplay between them explains a vast range of cognitive errors, from misjudging probabilities to overpaying for insurance.

Kahneman described these modes as System 1 and System 2. System 1 is fast, automatic, and effortless — it recognises faces, completes familiar phrases, and generates gut reactions without conscious input. System 2 is slow, deliberate, and effortful — it handles complex arithmetic, careful comparison, and logical reasoning. The critical insight is that System 2 is lazy. Because deliberate thinking requires energy, the brain defaults to System 1 for the vast majority of decisions. This works well in routine situations, but System 1 is vulnerable to systematic biases: it substitutes easy questions for hard ones, anchors on irrelevant numbers, and confuses cognitive fluency with truth. Classic demonstrations include the bat-and-ball problem (where intuition produces a confident but wrong answer) and the Linda problem (where people judge a conjunction of events as more likely than a single event because it fits a narrative).

For marketing and consumer behaviour, the dual-system framework explains why framing, priming, and default options are so powerful — they all target System 1, which processes most purchase decisions. Brands that feel familiar are trusted more, not because consumers have analysed their quality, but because System 1 equates ease of processing with safety. Pricing strategies like charm pricing (£9.99 vs £10) exploit System 1's shallow numerical processing. The framework also explains why rational persuasion often fails: detailed arguments engage System 2, but most consumers never activate it. Effective marketing speaks to System 1 first — through simplicity, emotion, and repetition — and only engages System 2 when the consumer is already leaning toward a decision.
