# Save More Tomorrow: Using Behavioral Economics to Increase Employee Saving (2004) — Thaler & Benartzi

Richard Thaler and Shlomo Benartzi tackled a stubborn problem: people consistently under-save for retirement even when they acknowledge they should save more. Traditional economic models assumed this was simply a preference — people valued present consumption over future security. But Thaler and Benartzi suspected the real barriers were psychological, not rational. Loss aversion made every increase in savings feel like a painful cut to current spending, inertia kept people locked into whatever default rate they started with, and lack of self-control meant good intentions rarely translated into action. The question was whether a programme could be designed to work with these biases rather than against them.

Their solution was the Save More Tomorrow (SMarT) programme, which asked employees to commit in advance to allocating a portion of each future pay rise toward retirement savings. The design was elegantly aligned with behavioural principles: because the increases came from future raises rather than current income, participants never experienced a reduction in take-home pay — eliminating the bite of loss aversion. Because commitment was made well before the raise arrived, present bias and procrastination were sidestepped. And because enrolment was maintained through automatic escalation with an opt-out option, inertia worked in the saver's favour rather than against it. In the first implementation at a mid-size manufacturing company, participants who joined SMarT increased their savings rate from 3.5% to 13.6% over four annual pay rises — nearly quadrupling their contribution without ever feeling a sacrifice.

The SMarT programme demonstrated that the same psychological forces that cause under-saving — loss aversion, inertia, present bias — can be redirected to produce dramatically better outcomes. For marketers and product designers, the principle extends well beyond pensions. Subscription models that lock in future upgrades, fitness apps that commit users to tomorrow's habits, and pricing structures that escalate gradually all exploit the same insight: people will accept future costs they would reject today. The study proved that the most effective behaviour change strategies do not fight human psychology — they channel it.
