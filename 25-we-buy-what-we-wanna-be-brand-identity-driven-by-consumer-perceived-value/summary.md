# We Buy What We Wanna Be: Brand Identity Driven by Consumer Perceived Value (2022) — Xi et al.

When a consumer chooses Nike over Adidas or Apple over Samsung, is the decision really about product quality — or is it about who they want to be? Xi and colleagues investigated the link between self-image and brand choice, asking whether consumers systematically select brands whose identity matches their ideal self rather than their actual self. This question mattered because if purchase decisions are driven by identity aspiration rather than rational evaluation, then traditional marketing approaches focused on features and price comparisons would be missing the real mechanism behind consumer preference.

The researchers built a model connecting three types of perceived brand value — functional (does it work well?), emotional (does it feel good?), and social (does it make me look good to others?) — to purchase intention, with self-congruity acting as the bridge. Self-congruity is the degree of overlap between a consumer's ideal self-image and the image projected by the brand. Through survey data and structural equation modelling, they found that all three value types predicted purchase intention, but the effect was significantly stronger when the brand's identity aligned with who the consumer wanted to become. Emotional and social value were particularly powerful predictors — more so than functional value — suggesting that feelings and identity carried more weight in brand choice than objective product performance. The mediating role of self-congruity meant that even a functionally superior product could lose to a competitor that better matched the consumer's aspirational identity.

For marketers, this study reinforces that brand-building is fundamentally about identity construction. Brands that succeed are not just selling products; they are offering consumers a mirror that reflects their ideal selves. This explains why brand storytelling, lifestyle imagery, and aspirational ambassadors drive purchase behaviour more effectively than spec sheets and feature lists. It also means that consumer segmentation should include psychographic profiles — who do your customers want to be? — alongside traditional demographic and behavioural data. A brand that answers the question "does this feel like me at my best?" wins over one that merely answers "does this work well?"
