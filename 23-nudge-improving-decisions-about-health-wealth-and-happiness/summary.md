# Nudge: Improving Decisions About Health, Wealth, and Happiness (2008) — Thaler & Sunstein

Every day, people make choices inside environments that someone else designed — a cafeteria layout, a website checkout flow, a pension enrolment form. Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein asked a question that reshaped public policy and marketing: what if the person designing that environment could steer decisions simply by rearranging how options are presented, without removing any choices or changing any incentives? This mattered because traditional economics assumed people made rational decisions regardless of how options were laid out. Thaler and Sunstein argued that this was wrong — presentation shapes outcomes profoundly — and that this power could be used deliberately for good.

Their framework, which they called "libertarian paternalism," centres on the concept of a "choice architect": anyone who organises the context in which people decide. The book catalogues specific nudge techniques and the evidence behind each. Default options are the most powerful — when organ donation is opt-out rather than opt-in, participation jumps from roughly 15% to over 85% across European countries. Framing effects show that describing a surgery as having a "90% survival rate" versus a "10% mortality rate" changes patient consent decisions. Social norm messaging — telling hotel guests that "75% of guests in this room reused their towels" — increases towel reuse far more than environmental appeals. Simplification nudges, like auto-enrolment in pension schemes, increased savings participation in the United States from 49% to 86%. Crucially, none of these interventions restrict freedom; every original option remains available.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, the implications are enormous. Every product page, pricing table, subscription form, and retail layout is a choice architecture. The order of menu items, which option is pre-selected, what social proof is displayed, and how information is framed all function as nudges that shift consumer behaviour at scale. Companies that understand nudge theory can increase conversions, reduce churn, and guide healthier customer relationships — not by persuading harder, but by designing the decision environment more thoughtfully.
