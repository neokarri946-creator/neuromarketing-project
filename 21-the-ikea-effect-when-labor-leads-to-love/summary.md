# The IKEA Effect: When Labor Leads to Love (2012) — Norton, Mochon & Ariely

Why do people cherish a wobbly bookshelf they assembled themselves more than a flawless one from a showroom? Michael Norton, Daniel Mochon, and Dan Ariely set out to test whether the act of building something — even badly — inflates how much people think it is worth. The question had real stakes for business: if labour reliably boosts perceived value, then companies could charge more simply by making customers do some of the work. It also challenged the standard economic assumption that consumers are rational appraisers of quality. If self-made products are over-valued, it would reveal a systematic bias wired into human judgement.

Across four experiments, participants assembled IKEA storage boxes, folded origami figures, or built sets of LEGO. They were then asked how much they would pay to keep the items they had made, compared with identical items built by someone else. The results were striking: builders consistently valued their own creations around 63% more than non-builders valued the same objects — even though independent judges rated the self-made items as objectively worse. Crucially, the effect depended on completion. When participants started but were prevented from finishing, the valuation boost disappeared. This ruled out simple ownership or "mere touch" explanations and pointed instead to the psychological reward of seeing a task through to the end. The researchers named the phenomenon the "IKEA Effect" after the furniture retailer whose flat-pack model depends on exactly this dynamic.

For marketers, the implications are practical and powerful. Customisation tools, build-your-own product kits, and co-creation platforms all exploit the IKEA Effect by giving consumers a sense of authorship. Nike By You, Build-A-Bear, and meal-kit services succeed partly because customers invest effort and therefore value the outcome more than an equivalent ready-made product. The finding also warns against removing all friction from the consumer journey — some effort, when it leads to a completed result, can actually increase satisfaction and willingness to pay rather than diminish it.
