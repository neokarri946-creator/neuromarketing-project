# Tesco (Clubcard)

## What They Do

Tesco is the UK's biggest supermarket chain. Their Clubcard loyalty programme gives customers points every time they shop. Those points can be spent on discounts, vouchers, and "Clubcard Prices" — special lower prices only Clubcard holders get.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### Endowment Effect

- **What it is:** Once you own something — or feel like you own it — you value it more than you would if you didn't have it. Even if it's just some points on a card.
- **How Tesco uses it:** Your Clubcard points feel like "your money." You've earned them through shopping. Even though the points might only be worth a few pounds, people treat them carefully — because losing something you already "have" feels worse than never having it.
- **Study connection:** Study 10 — Anomalies (endowment effect)

### Loss Aversion

- **What it is:** Losing something hurts roughly twice as much as gaining the same thing feels good. We go out of our way to avoid losses.
- **How Tesco uses it:** Tesco sends emails saying "Your points expire soon — use them before you lose them!" This triggers loss aversion. You're not motivated by what you could gain — you're motivated by the fear of losing points you already have. It drives people back into the store.
- **Study connection:** Study 08 — Prospect Theory (loss aversion)

### Mental Accounting

- **What it is:** People treat money differently depending on where it comes from or what it's labelled as. £10 in "Clubcard savings" feels different from £10 in your bank account — even though it's the same amount.
- **How Tesco uses it:** "Clubcard Prices" feel like a separate discount world. When shoppers see a Clubcard Price of £2.50 instead of £3.50, it feels like they're spending from a special "discount budget" — not their real money. This makes people buy things they might not have bought otherwise.
- **Study connection:** Study 12 — Mental Accounting

### Commitment and Consistency

- **What it is:** Once you've committed to something — even something small — you feel pressure to keep acting consistently with that commitment.
- **How Tesco uses it:** Signing up for a Clubcard is a small commitment. But once you have the card, you keep scanning it every shop. Over time, shopping at Tesco becomes a habit — switching to Sainsbury's would mean "wasting" your Clubcard progress.
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's commitment and consistency principle)

### Data-Driven Personalisation

- **What it is:** Using your past behaviour to predict what you'll want next — and putting it right in front of you.
- **How Tesco uses it:** Tesco tracks every purchase you make. They send personalised coupons for products you regularly buy — "50p off your usual milk." This feels helpful, but it's also a nudge. It keeps you buying the same things at Tesco instead of going elsewhere.
- **Study connection:** Study 23 — Nudge (choice architecture)

## Why It Works

The Clubcard creates a loop that's hard to break. You earn points (endowment), you don't want to lose them (loss aversion), you see special prices as "bonus savings" (mental accounting), and the habit keeps you coming back (commitment). Tesco doesn't just sell groceries — they make leaving feel like a loss.
