# Supermarkets

## What They Do

Supermarkets like Tesco, Sainsbury's, Asda, and Aldi sell everyday groceries and household products. They look simple, but every detail — from shelf height to aisle layout — is designed to make you spend more than you planned.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### Choice Architecture (shelf placement and store layout)

- **What it is:** The way options are arranged influences what you pick — without removing any choices.
- **How supermarkets use it:** Expensive branded products sit at eye level. Cheaper own-brand items go on the bottom shelf where you have to bend down. End-of-aisle displays catch your attention as you turn corners. Essentials like milk and bread are placed at the back, forcing you to walk past everything else.
- **Study connection:** Study 23 — Nudge (choice architecture)

### Defaults (meal deals and pre-selected bundles)

- **What it is:** People tend to stick with whatever option is already chosen for them. Defaults are powerful because changing them takes effort.
- **How supermarkets use it:** Tesco's £3.50 meal deal bundles a sandwich, drink, and snack together. You could buy items separately (sometimes cheaper), but the default bundle feels easy and like a "deal." You take the default without questioning it.
- **Study connection:** Study 30 — Do Defaults Save Lives

### Anchoring ("was" prices)

- **What it is:** The first number you see sets a mental anchor. Everything after is judged against it.
- **How supermarkets use it:** "Was £4.50, now £3.00" makes £3 feel like a steal — even if the product was only ever worth £3. The "was" price is the anchor. Supermarkets use this on yellow stickers, promotional signs, and Clubcard prices constantly.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (anchoring)

### Nudges (healthier choices at eye level)

- **What it is:** A nudge gently steers people toward a better choice without banning the bad one.
- **How supermarkets use it:** Some stores place fruit and vegetables right at the entrance, so the first thing you see is healthy food. Water is placed at eye level in the drinks aisle, while sugary drinks sit lower. These small design choices shift behaviour without anyone noticing.
- **Study connection:** Study 23 — Nudge

### Scarcity ("while stocks last")

- **What it is:** Limited availability creates urgency. If you think it might run out, you grab it now.
- **How supermarkets use it:** Aldi and Lidl's "Special Buys" / "Middle of Lidl" are only available in limited quantities. Signs saying "while stocks last" or "limited edition" push you to buy immediately instead of thinking it over.
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's scarcity principle)

### Mental Accounting (budget categories feel separate)

- **What it is:** People mentally divide their money into separate "accounts" — like food, treats, household — even though it's all the same money.
- **How supermarkets use it:** You might set a £50 weekly food budget, but treats, cleaning products, and snacks feel like they come from "different" budgets. Supermarkets encourage this by separating sections clearly. That £4 chocolate bar doesn't feel like it's eating into your food budget — it's from your "treat" account.
- **Study connection:** Study 12 — Mental Accounting

## Why It Works

Supermarkets control the environment you shop in. They decide what you see first, what sits at eye level, and how prices are framed. You think you're making free choices, but the architecture of the store is quietly steering every decision you make.
