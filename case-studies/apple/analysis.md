# Apple

## What They Do

Apple makes phones, laptops, tablets, and other tech products. They are famous for their premium branding — people see Apple as a luxury, high-quality brand. Their marketing is just as carefully designed as their products.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### Anchoring

- **What it is:** When you see a big number first, everything after it feels cheaper by comparison. The first price you see becomes your "anchor" — your brain judges everything else against it.
- **How Apple uses it:** When Apple launches a new iPhone, they show the most expensive model first (e.g. iPhone 16 Pro Max at £1,199). After seeing that, the standard iPhone 16 at £799 feels like a bargain — even though £799 is still a lot of money.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (anchoring heuristic) and Study 20 — Predictably Irrational (anchoring)

### Decoy Effect

- **What it is:** When you're choosing between two options and a third option is added that makes one of the originals look like a much better deal.
- **How Apple uses it:** Apple offers three storage tiers — say 128GB, 256GB, and 512GB. The 128GB feels too small, the 512GB feels too expensive, so most people pick the 256GB in the middle. That's exactly what Apple wants.
- **Study connection:** Study 20 — Predictably Irrational (decoy effect)

### Scarcity

- **What it is:** When something feels rare or limited, we want it more. Our brain panics a little — "I might miss out!"
- **How Apple uses it:** At every product launch, stock is limited. Long waiting lists and "ships in 4-6 weeks" messages make people rush to order. The queues outside Apple Stores on launch day add to the feeling.
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's scarcity principle)

### IKEA Effect

- **What it is:** We value things more when we've put effort into making them ourselves — even if the effort is small.
- **How Apple uses it:** Apple lets you engrave AirPods, customise your Apple Watch bands, and pick your MacBook specs. Because you've "built" your own version, it feels more personal and more valuable to you.
- **Study connection:** Study 21 — IKEA Effect

### Social Proof

- **What it is:** We look at what other people are doing to decide what we should do. If everyone else is buying it, it must be good.
- **How Apple uses it:** The famous queues outside Apple Stores are social proof in action. Seeing hundreds of people lining up signals: "This product is worth waiting for." Apple also highlights sales numbers — "Over 1 billion iPhones sold."
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's social proof principle)

## Why It Works

Apple layers multiple tactics on top of each other. The anchoring makes prices feel reasonable, the scarcity creates urgency, the social proof reassures you, and the IKEA effect makes you feel ownership before you've even paid. Together, these nudge you towards buying — and feeling good about it.
