# Uber

## What They Do

Uber is a ride-hailing app. You request a car on your phone, a driver picks you up, and you pay through the app. They also use "surge pricing" — prices go up when demand is high, like on a Friday night or during rain.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### Anchoring and Surge Pricing

- **What it is:** Anchoring means the first number you see shapes your expectations. When you're used to a normal price, any increase feels like a comparison against that anchor.
- **How Uber uses it:** On a normal day, your ride might cost £8. During surge pricing, it jumps to £16. But Uber shows you the surge multiplier — "1.5x" or "2x" — alongside the original estimate. The normal price is the anchor, making you aware of exactly what you're "overpaying." Ironically, this often makes people accept the surge because they understand the logic.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (anchoring)

### Loss Aversion for Drivers

- **What it is:** People work harder to avoid losing something than to gain something of equal value.
- **How Uber uses it:** Uber sends drivers messages like "You're only £5 away from earning £100 today." This frames stopping as a loss — you'd be "giving up" the £100 milestone. Drivers keep driving longer than they planned, because quitting feels like losing money they almost had.
- **Study connection:** Study 08 — Prospect Theory (loss aversion) and Study 09 — Choices, Values, and Frames (framing)

### Scarcity as a Signal

- **What it is:** When something is scarce, we assume it must be valuable or in high demand.
- **How Uber uses it:** Surge pricing itself acts as a scarcity signal. When you see "prices are higher due to high demand," your brain reads that as: "Cars are scarce right now — I'd better book now before it gets worse." The high price doesn't push you away — it pushes you to act fast.
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's scarcity principle)

### Status Quo Bias (Default Settings)

- **What it is:** People tend to stick with whatever option is pre-selected for them, because changing it requires effort and feels risky.
- **How Uber uses it:** Your payment method is saved and auto-selected. Tip amounts are pre-set at suggested percentages (e.g. 10%, 15%, 20%). Most riders just tap the default tip without thinking. The default does the choosing for you.
- **Study connection:** Study 32 — Status Quo Bias and Study 23 — Nudge (defaults)

### Nudges and Framing

- **What it is:** Small design choices that steer behaviour without restricting freedom. How you frame a situation changes how people react.
- **How Uber uses it:** When you open the app, it shows a map with cars moving around near you. This is a nudge — it makes you think "a car is right there, I might as well book one." The visual of nearby cars reduces hesitation and makes ordering feel effortless.
- **Study connection:** Study 23 — Nudge (choice architecture) and Study 09 — Choices, Values, and Frames

## Why It Works

Uber controls both sides — riders and drivers — using behavioural economics. Riders are nudged to book quickly through scarcity and defaults. Drivers are nudged to keep working through loss aversion framing. The app is designed so the easiest thing to do is exactly what Uber wants: book more rides and drive more hours.
