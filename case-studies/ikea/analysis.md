# IKEA

## What They Do

IKEA sells affordable, flat-pack furniture that you assemble yourself. They operate huge warehouse-style stores and a growing online shop. They're one of the biggest furniture retailers in the world.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### The IKEA Effect (you build it, you love it)

- **What it is:** When you put effort into making something, you value it more than if you'd just bought it ready-made. Even if it's wobbly.
- **How IKEA uses it:** Their entire model is built on this. You buy a BILLY bookcase for £30, spend an hour assembling it, and suddenly it feels like *your* creation. You're less likely to replace it or switch to a competitor.
- **Study connection:** Study 21 — The IKEA Effect

### Store Layout as Choice Architecture (the one-way maze)

- **What it is:** Choice architecture means designing the environment so people are guided toward certain decisions — without forcing them.
- **How IKEA uses it:** Their stores follow a one-way path that takes you through every single department. You came for a lamp, but you walk past kitchens, bedrooms, and living rooms first. The layout nudges you to see (and buy) things you didn't plan to.
- **Study connection:** Study 23 — Nudge (choice architecture and defaults)

### Anchoring (expensive items first)

- **What it is:** The first price you see becomes your mental "anchor." Everything after it feels cheap by comparison.
- **How IKEA uses it:** In each section, the first items you see are the premium range (e.g., a £400 sofa). By the time you reach the £150 one, it feels like a bargain — even though £150 is still a lot of money.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (anchoring)

### Scarcity (limited editions)

- **What it is:** When something is rare or running out, we want it more. Scarcity creates urgency.
- **How IKEA uses it:** They release limited edition collections (like designer collaborations with Virgil Abloh or Marimekko) that are only available for a short time. People queue up or buy immediately because they know it won't last.
- **Study connection:** Study 13 — Influence (Cialdini's scarcity principle)

### Loss Aversion ("last chance" sales)

- **What it is:** We hate losing something more than we enjoy gaining something of equal value. The pain of missing out is stronger than the joy of saving.
- **How IKEA uses it:** "Last Chance" signs appear on items being discontinued. The framing isn't "here's a deal" — it's "you'll lose the chance to ever buy this." That loss framing pushes people to buy now.
- **Study connection:** Study 08 — Prospect Theory (loss aversion)

## Why It Works

IKEA layers multiple tactics on top of each other. The store layout pulls you in, anchoring makes prices feel reasonable, and the assembly process makes you emotionally attached to what you buy. It's a system designed to make you spend more — and feel good about it.
