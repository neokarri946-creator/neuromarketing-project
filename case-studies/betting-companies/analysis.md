# Betting Companies (Bet365, Sky Bet, etc.)

## What They Do

Betting companies let you gamble on sports, casino games, and other events through apps and websites. The UK gambling industry is worth over £14 billion a year. Companies like Bet365, Sky Bet, and Paddy Power dominate the market.

## Behavioural Economics Tactics They Use

### Variable Rewards (the dopamine hit)

- **What it is:** Unpredictable rewards are far more addictive than predictable ones. Your brain releases dopamine not when you win, but when you *might* win.
- **How they use it:** Every bet is a variable reward. You don't know if you'll win or lose, and that uncertainty is what keeps you coming back. Slot machines, live in-play betting, and accumulators all amplify this — the outcome is always unknown.
- **Study connection:** Study 07 — Dopamine Economy

### Loss Chasing (risk-seeking after losses)

- **What it is:** Prospect Theory says people become risk-seeking when they're losing. Instead of cutting losses, they gamble more to try to "get back to even."
- **How they use it:** Betting apps make it incredibly easy to place another bet immediately after losing. "Cash out" features and in-play betting keep you engaged in the moment. The design removes any pause for reflection — you chase your losses before you've even processed them.
- **Study connection:** Study 08 — Prospect Theory (loss aversion and risk-seeking)

### Optimism Bias ("I'll win next time")

- **What it is:** People consistently overestimate their chances of good outcomes and underestimate the bad ones. We think we're luckier than average.
- **How they use it:** Betting apps show your wins prominently in your history but bury the losses. "Bet builders" let you customise bets, which makes you feel like you have skill and control — even though the odds are still against you.
- **Study connection:** Study 39 — Unrealistic Optimism

### Anchoring (big jackpot numbers)

- **What it is:** A large number shown first makes everything else feel relative to it. It sets a mental "starting point."
- **How they use it:** Bet365 shows jackpot figures like "£1,000,000" on their homepage. Accumulator odds like "500/1" are displayed prominently. These huge numbers anchor your expectations — you start imagining the big win, not the likely loss.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (anchoring)

### Free Bets as Foot-in-the-Door

- **What it is:** A small free gift gets your foot in the door. Once you've started, you're more likely to continue with your own money.
- **How they use it:** "Bet £10, get £30 in free bets" is the classic offer. It feels like free money. But to use the free bets, you have to sign up, deposit real money, and start gambling. Most people don't stop after the free bets run out.
- **Study connection:** Study 15 — Foot-in-the-Door Technique

### Availability Heuristic (winners, not losers)

- **What it is:** We judge how likely something is based on how easily we can think of examples. If we keep seeing winners, we think winning is common.
- **How they use it:** Adverts show people celebrating big wins. "Ray won £50,000 on an accumulator!" is everywhere. You never see the millions who lost. This makes winning feel achievable and common — when statistically, it isn't.
- **Study connection:** Study 29 — Heuristics and Biases (availability heuristic)

## Why It Works

Betting companies exploit how our brains process risk, reward, and loss. They make winning feel likely, losing feel temporary, and stopping feel impossible. These aren't accidents — they're carefully designed systems that keep people gambling.
