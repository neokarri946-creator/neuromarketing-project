# Attitudinal Effects of Mere Exposure (1968) — Zajonc

Why do people prefer familiar things? Before Robert Zajonc's landmark 1968 paper, psychologists generally assumed that preferences were built through conscious evaluation — people liked things because they had reasoned their way to a positive judgement. Zajonc proposed something far more radical: that simple repeated exposure to a stimulus, with no evaluation, no reward, and no conscious thought required, was enough to make people like it more. If true, this would mean that a significant portion of human preference had nothing to do with quality, logic, or experience — it was simply a product of frequency.

Zajonc tested this across a series of elegant experiments using nonsense words, Chinese characters, and photographs of faces — stimuli that participants had no prior associations with. Each stimulus was shown a different number of times, ranging from zero to twenty-five exposures. Crucially, participants were not asked to remember the stimuli or learn anything about them — the exposures were incidental. When later asked to rate how much they liked each stimulus, participants consistently rated the most frequently shown items as more pleasant, more positive, and more attractive. The effect was robust across all stimulus types and occurred even when participants could not consciously recognise which items they had seen more often. Familiarity did not just breed tolerance — it actively generated liking, and it operated below the threshold of conscious awareness.

The mere exposure effect became one of the most replicated findings in psychology and one of the most exploited in marketing. It explains why brands spend billions on advertising that contains no persuasive argument — simply being seen repeatedly is enough to increase preference. It underpins product placement, brand logos on sports jerseys, and jingles that play in the background. For consumer behaviour, the implication is profound: the first battle in marketing is not persuasion but presence. A brand seen more often will be preferred more, even if consumers cannot articulate why, making share of attention arguably more valuable than share of argument.
