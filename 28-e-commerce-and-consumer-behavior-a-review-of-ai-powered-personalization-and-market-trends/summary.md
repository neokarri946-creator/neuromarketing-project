# E-commerce and Consumer Behavior: A Review of AI-Powered Personalization and Market Trends (2024)

As e-commerce has grown into a multi-trillion-pound global industry, artificial intelligence has become the invisible engine behind nearly every aspect of the online shopping experience — from what products appear on your screen to what price you see to when you receive a promotional email. This 2024 review examined how AI-powered personalisation systems actually work in e-commerce, what makes them effective, and where the tensions between commercial power and consumer welfare emerge. The question matters because these systems now influence the spending decisions of billions of people daily, yet most consumers have little understanding of the mechanisms shaping their choices.

The review identified three primary channels through which AI personalisation operates: recommendation engines that suggest products based on behavioural data, dynamic pricing algorithms that adjust prices in real time based on demand signals and individual willingness to pay, and targeted marketing that delivers customised advertisements and promotions. The effectiveness of all three depends on a shared set of conditions: the quality and breadth of data feeding the system, the transparency of the algorithms to both managers and consumers, and the balance struck between personalisation depth and privacy protection. Studies consistently showed that consumers respond positively to personalisation they perceive as helpful but negatively when they feel surveilled, manipulated, or unable to understand why certain content is being shown to them. Data quality proved especially critical — poorly trained algorithms that make irrelevant or repetitive recommendations erode trust faster than no personalisation at all.

For practitioners, the review makes clear that AI personalisation is not simply a technology problem but a trust architecture. The most successful e-commerce platforms treat data collection, algorithm design, and consumer communication as parts of a single system. They collect data with clear consent, explain recommendations in plain language, offer easy opt-out mechanisms, and resist the temptation to extract maximum short-term revenue from dynamic pricing at the cost of long-term loyalty. The review warns that as regulatory frameworks like the EU's AI Act tighten, companies that have built trust-first personalisation will hold a significant competitive advantage over those scrambling to retrofit transparency onto opaque systems.
