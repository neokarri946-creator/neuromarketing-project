# Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision Under Risk (1979) — Kahneman & Tversky

Before 1979, economists assumed people made decisions by calculating expected value — multiplying each outcome's probability by its magnitude and choosing the highest number. Daniel Kahneman and Amos Tversky challenged this assumption head-on, asking how people actually make decisions when outcomes are uncertain. The question mattered because real human choices consistently violated the predictions of expected utility theory: people bought insurance and lottery tickets simultaneously, avoided fair gambles, and treated small probabilities in wildly inconsistent ways. A new theory was needed to explain what decision-making actually looks like inside the human mind.

Through a series of carefully designed choice experiments, Kahneman and Tversky documented three fundamental patterns. First, people evaluate outcomes as gains or losses relative to a reference point, not as final states of wealth — losing $100 from your current position feels different from having $100 less in total. Second, losses loom larger than gains: the pain of losing $100 is roughly twice as intense as the pleasure of gaining $100, creating a powerful asymmetry called loss aversion. Third, people are risk-averse when facing gains (preferring a sure $500 over a 50% chance of $1,000) but risk-seeking when facing losses (preferring a 50% chance of losing $1,000 over a certain loss of $500). The authors formalised these patterns into prospect theory, complete with a value function that is steeper for losses than gains and an S-shaped probability weighting function that overweights small probabilities and underweights large ones.

Prospect theory transformed marketing, finance, and public policy. It explains why "money-back guarantees" are so effective (they eliminate loss), why consumers prefer $5 off over 5% off even when the latter is worth more, and why investors hold losing stocks too long while selling winners too quickly. The theory earned Kahneman the Nobel Prize in Economics in 2002 and remains the most cited alternative to classical decision theory, providing the foundation for behavioural economics as a discipline.
