# Anticipation of Increasing Monetary Reward Selectively Recruits Nucleus Accumbens (2001) — Knutson et al.

What makes the human reward system light up — is it getting what you want, or the moment just before you get it? Brian Knutson and colleagues at Stanford posed this question in 2001 using fMRI to separate the neural responses to anticipating a reward from the responses to actually receiving it. This distinction mattered enormously because if the brain's pleasure circuitry is driven more by anticipation than by consumption, it would reshape our understanding of motivation, addiction, and consumer behaviour. The dominant assumption had been that reward response peaks at the moment of receiving something valuable.

The researchers designed a monetary incentive delay task where participants saw a cue indicating they could win varying amounts of money ($0.20, $1.00, or $5.00), waited through a brief anticipation period, then performed a quick reaction-time task to claim the reward. The fMRI data revealed a striking dissociation. The nucleus accumbens — a core structure in the brain's reward circuit — responded most powerfully during the anticipation phase, and its activation scaled proportionally with the size of the expected reward. Larger anticipated rewards produced significantly greater nucleus accumbens activity. By contrast, the actual receipt of the reward activated different regions, particularly the medial prefrontal cortex. The wanting and the getting were neurologically distinct events, and the wanting produced the stronger dopaminergic signal.

For marketing and consumer behaviour, this finding explains why teasers, countdowns, pre-orders, and "coming soon" campaigns are so effective — they exploit the brain's anticipation circuitry, which generates more neural excitement than the product delivery itself. It explains why unboxing videos generate massive engagement, why limited-time offers create urgency that feels physically exciting, and why the anticipation of a holiday often brings more happiness than the holiday itself. Marketers who understand this invest heavily in building desire before purchase, knowing that the journey toward a reward is neurologically more powerful than arrival.
