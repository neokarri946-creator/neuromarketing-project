# Do Defaults Save Lives? (2003) — Johnson & Goldstein

In many European countries, more than 90% of citizens are registered organ donors. In others, fewer than 15% are. The difference is not wealth, education, religion, or culture — it is a single bureaucratic detail: whether the form requires you to opt in or opt out. Eric Johnson and Daniel Goldstein asked whether default settings could genuinely explain such enormous differences in a decision as consequential as organ donation, and the answer has reshaped how policymakers, marketers, and designers think about choice architecture.

The researchers compared real-world organ donation rates across European countries and conducted controlled experiments where participants were assigned to opt-in, opt-out, or neutral default conditions. The results were striking and consistent. Countries with opt-out defaults (you are a donor unless you actively remove yourself) had donation consent rates near 100%, while opt-in countries (you must actively sign up) hovered between 4% and 27%. In the lab experiments, participants given an opt-out default were roughly twice as likely to remain donors compared to those given an opt-in default — even though everyone was free to change their selection. The effect persisted regardless of how people felt about donation: it was not that opt-out countries had more altruistic citizens, but that the default was simply sticky. People tend to accept whatever option is pre-selected, partly through inertia, partly because they interpret the default as the recommended or normal choice, and partly because making an active change requires effort and triggers loss aversion.

For anyone designing systems where people make choices — product subscriptions, privacy settings, retirement plans, consent forms — this study delivers a blunt message: the default option is not neutral. It is one of the most powerful tools available for shaping behaviour at scale. Setting a default is effectively making the choice for the majority of people, because most will never change it. This places an ethical responsibility on designers and policymakers: if defaults are going to determine outcomes whether you think about them or not, they should be set deliberately, transparently, and with the welfare of the chooser in mind.
